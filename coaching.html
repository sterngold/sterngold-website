Reflection

The concept

The Promethean Gap

In 1956, philosopher Günther Anders described something factory workers already felt: the shame of being slower, less precise, and less reliable than the machines they operated. He called it Prometheische Scham — Promethean shame.

Seventy years later, it’s happening at standing desks. When an AI writes faster than you, summarizes better than you, and doesn’t need coffee — the gap between what you build and what you can feel about what you’ve built becomes unbearable.

That gap is the Promethean Gap. And it’s the central problem of AI transformation that nobody is talking about in the vendor decks.

The origin

Why “Anders”

Günther Anders was born Günther Stern. My family name is Sterngold. The connection is coincidence — and also not.

Anders means “different” in Dutch and German. WerkAnders = work, differently. It’s a philosophy first, a company second. The work I do starts from the premise that transformation isn’t a technology problem — it’s a human one.

The framework

How I work with executives

Name the feeling

Most executives can describe the technology. Few can describe what it does to them. The first step is language.

Map the gap

Where is the distance between what your organization can build and what your people can absorb? That’s where the breakdowns happen.

Build the bridge

Not a training program. Not a change management deck. A human conversation about identity, fear, and what comes after the old role disappears.

Lead through it

Transformation isn’t a phase — it’s a permanent state. The goal isn’t to survive it. It’s to lead from inside it.

Where I see it

The Steering Committee

Danone, 2022

The marketing mix model was ready. Months of work. The data lead — the one who’d actually built it — sat at the back of the room. The committee debated the percentage. No one asked her what it meant.

The person who understood the machine became invisible. That’s not a technology failure. That’s a Promethean failure.

The Mole Scanner

Amsterdam, 2025

207 moles. Nine minutes. The AI scanner photographed, measured, and classified every one. Then the doctor looked at one mole the machine had flagged as benign and said: “I want to see that one again in three months.”

The machine processed pixels. The doctor read a body. What happens to that knowledge when we optimize for speed?

The Translator

Booking.com, 2011–2020

For nine years, my job was bridging the gap between technical teams and business leadership. I built the team that automated customer service. I helped launch machine translation across ten languages.

Then AI made the translation unnecessary. The thing I’d built replaced the role I’d carved out. That’s not someone else’s story. That’s mine.
