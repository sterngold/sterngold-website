The Promethean Gap

What happens to people when machines get better than them

— Vlad Sterngold

The Origin

In 1956, a German-Jewish philosopher named Günther Anders sat in his apartment in New York and wrote about factory workers in Vienna. They were ashamed. Not of what they'd done — of what they couldn't do. The machines on their production lines were faster, more precise, more reliable. The workers looked at them and felt something no one had a name for.

Anders named it. Prometheische Scham — Promethean shame. The feeling that strikes when the thing you made is better than you. It was 1956. The machines in question were mechanical looms.

Now the machines write code, compose music, diagnose disease, and negotiate contracts. The shame Anders described hasn't disappeared. It's moved from the factory floor to the open-plan office. From the loom operator to the marketing director. From Vienna to everywhere.

I've spent fifteen years watching it happen — at Booking.com, at Danone, in boardrooms across Europe. The gap Anders measured between what humans build and what humans can grasp about what they've built is no longer a philosophical thought experiment. It's the central challenge of every organization I've worked with.

This research draws on Anders' framework and extends it with the work of Joy Buolamwini, who proved the gap is measurable, and Alice Guy-Blaché, who showed — a century before AI — that outsiders see what inventors can't.

The Framework

Three concepts. One gap. Each builds on the last.

🔥

Promethean Shame

The feeling

The moment when the machine is faster than you. You've felt it. The intern who uses ChatGPT to do in ten minutes what took you a career to learn. The spreadsheet that renders your expertise decorative. Anders named it in 1956. It hasn't aged a day.

“Man is ashamed of having been born instead of made.”
— Günther Anders, The Obsolescence of Man, 1956

📏

The Promethean Gap

The structure

The structural distance between what we make and what we understand about what we've made. It's not abstract — Joy Buolamwini proved it's encoded. Her research showed facial recognition that works on white faces and fails on dark ones. The gap isn't philosophical. It's in the training data.

“If you have a face, you have a place in this conversation.”
— Joy Buolamwini, Algorithmic Justice League, 2018

👁

Promethean Blindness

The inability to see

The failure to see what's happening while it's happening. Cinema took thirty years to become art. The Lumière brothers filmed a train arriving and thought they'd invented a toy. Alice Guy-Blaché — a secretary at Gaumont — saw a story. She directed over a thousand films. Then history erased her.

“There is nothing connected with the staging of a motion picture that a woman cannot do as easily as a man.”
— Alice Guy-Blaché, 1914

The Promethean Gap is the space between what humans can build and what humans can feel about what they've built. It shows up as shame, blindness, and the quiet moment when someone realizes the machine doesn't need them anymore.

Where I See It

Three moments from fifteen years of watching this gap up close.

The Steering Committee

Danone, 2022

The marketing mix model was ready. Months of work. The data lead from Belgium — the one who'd actually built it, who could code, who understood every coefficient — sat at the back of the room. The steering committee looked at the dashboard and debated the percentage. No one asked her what it meant.

The percentage became the decision. The person who understood the machine became invisible. That's not a technology failure. That's a Promethean failure — the gap between building the tool and understanding what it's telling you.

The Mole Scanner

Amsterdam, 2025

207 moles. Nine minutes. The AI scanner photographed, measured, and classified every one. Faster than any dermatologist. More consistent. More thorough. Then the doctor looked at one mole the machine had flagged as benign and said: “I want to see that one again in three months.”

The machine processed pixels. The doctor read a body. Thirty years of looking at skin, at patients, at the way a person flinches when you ask about sun exposure. What happens to that knowledge when we optimize for speed?

The Translator

Booking.com, 2011–2020

For nine years, my job was bridging the gap between technical teams and business leadership. I spoke both languages — data and strategy, engineering and boardroom. I built the team that automated customer service. I helped launch machine translation across ten languages.

Then AI made the translation unnecessary. The thing I'd built replaced the role I'd carved out. What happens to the bridge when both sides speak the same language? That's not someone else's story. That's mine.

The Question

Who is the person in your organization that the steering committee can't see?

They might be there right now — too quiet for the boardroom, too technical for the C-suite, too creative for the people who decide what counts.

In 1896, Alice Guy-Blaché was a secretary. She asked to borrow a camera. Within a year she was directing films. Over a thousand of them. Then history forgot her name.

That person in your organization? They're your AI pioneer.
And you're about to lose them.

About the Researcher

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.

The kitchens taught him that good work is physical before it's intellectual. The military taught him that systems break people before they break machines. Fifteen years of corporate transformation taught him that the gap Anders described in 1956 isn't closing — it's widening.

Based in Amsterdam. Writing the field notes. Still looking for the people the steering committee can't see.
