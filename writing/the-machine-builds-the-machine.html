The Machine That Builds the Machine

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

In 1956, Günther Anders made a prediction that should have been impossible. He said machines would stop waiting for us. That they would merge into a single system — he called it the "total machine" — and that system would begin to expand on its own terms, at its own pace, for its own reasons. Humans, he wrote, would become "co-historical": still present, still watching, but no longer the ones deciding what happens next.

He was describing a factory floor. He could have been describing last Tuesday.

I was in a meeting recently where someone demonstrated an AI agent. Not a chatbot — an agent. It reviewed a dataset, decided what was missing, wrote code to fetch the missing data, ran the code, analyzed the results, and produced a recommendation. When the recommendation didn't satisfy certain criteria, it revised its own approach and tried again.

Nobody told it to do that second part. The system corrected itself.

The room went quiet. Not the impressed quiet of "look what we built." A different quiet. The quiet of people realizing they were watching something that didn't need them to watch.

Anders had a word for what that room was feeling. He called it the condition of the "inverted Utopian." Traditional utopians imagine what they cannot yet build. We are the opposite. We build what we cannot imagine the consequences of. Every self-improving AI agent is proof: we set it in motion, and we cannot picture where it goes.

He called the numbness that follows "apocalyptic blindness" — not because the apocalypse is coming, but because our feelings aren't large enough to register what we've made. The machine isn't too dangerous to understand. It's too big to feel.

I've spent fifteen years in digital leadership at Booking.com and Danone. I've seen every technology cycle claim to be the one that changes everything. Most of them changed the tools and left the humans intact. This one is different. Not because AI is smarter — because AI builds AI. The machine builds the machine.

Anders described something he called "subject-object inversion." Technology, he argued, has become the subject of history. We have become its object. We don't set the pace. We respond to it. We don't choose the direction. We adapt.

Donna Haraway saw the same boundary dissolving from the other side. In her Cyborg Manifesto, she argued that the line between human and machine was already a fiction — and that pretending otherwise was itself a political act. Anders mourned the inversion. Haraway said: the merger already happened. The question is who gets to define its terms.

Listen to how people in your organization talk about AI. "We need to keep up." "We can't fall behind." "We have to move faster." Every sentence puts the human in the reactive position. The machine leads. The human follows. That's not a communication problem. That's the inversion Anders described, playing out in real time in your leadership meetings.

The scariest part isn't that the machine is getting smarter. It's that it's getting faster at getting smarter, while we stay the same. Anders called the human body "morphologically constant" — we haven't meaningfully changed in millennia. The AI was retrained this morning. The gap between our biology and our technology isn't closing. It's accelerating.

And yet — here's what most people miss about Anders — he wasn't a pessimist. He was a trainer. He believed the gap could be narrowed. Not by building better machines, and not by understanding AI more deeply. By training the human capacity to imagine, to feel, to choose in the face of what we've created. He called these "moral stretching exercises." Treat your imagination like a muscle, he said. Because it is one. And it has atrophied.

The real question isn't whether AI will keep building AI. It will. The question is whether the humans in the room will stay conscious enough to notice what's changing — in the systems, and in themselves.

Because the machine that builds the machine doesn't need your permission. But it does need your attention.

When was the last time your team made a strategic decision about AI — without using AI to make it?

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.
