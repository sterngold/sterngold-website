What Tennis Taught Me About Sitting With Losing

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 3 min read

When you're down 2-5 in a set, your body wants to fix it. Hit harder. Go for the lines. Force winners you wouldn't normally try. Everything in you screams: do something big, right now.

That's exactly when you lose the set.

The discipline — the real skill that nobody teaches you — is to slow down. Stay in the point. Play the ball in front of you. Not the score, not the set, not the match. Just this ball, right now.

I've lost more games rushing than I ever lost playing steady. And I've watched the same thing happen in every boardroom I've worked in for 15 years.

When AI started showing up in executive conversations, something shifted. Not in the technology — in the people. Suddenly, leaders who'd spent decades building their expertise felt like they were down 2-5. The world was moving fast. The tools were getting smarter. And the instinct kicked in: rush. Upskill everyone. Launch an AI academy. Announce a transformation programme. Do something big, right now.

Most of it didn't land. Not because the initiatives were wrong — but because they were driven by panic, not by clarity. They were winner-down-the-line shots from the baseline. Flashy, urgent, and out by a foot.

I learned this in kitchens before I ever saw a boardroom — when service is falling apart, the chef who starts screaming makes it worse. The one who calls the next ticket calmly brings the whole brigade back. At Booking.com and Danone, the moments that actually moved things forward were the same — quieter. They started with someone saying: we don't fully understand this yet, and that's fine. Let's stay in the point. What's the actual problem we're solving today? Not the five-year vision. Today.

That's hard. Especially for senior people. You got where you are by being decisive, by having answers. Sitting with not knowing — admitting you're behind and choosing not to panic — feels like weakness. It's the opposite. It's the steadiest thing you can do.

In tennis, the comeback never starts with a spectacular shot. It starts with a solid return. Then another. You hold your serve. You stay in the game. And suddenly it's 4-5, and the pressure is on the other side.

The same is true for navigating AI. The leaders who get through this aren't the ones who made the biggest bets fastest. They're the ones who stayed calm when they felt behind. Who played the ball in front of them instead of swinging for a transformation they didn't understand yet.

The game isn't over at 2-5. It's over when you stop playing steady.

Where in your work are you rushing a shot because the score feels uncomfortable?
