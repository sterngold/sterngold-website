What Tennis Taught Me About Sitting With Losing

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 3 min read

When you’re down 2-5 in a set, your body wants to fix it. Hit harder. Go for the lines. Force winners you wouldn’t normally try. Everything in you screams: do something big, right now.

That’s exactly when you lose the set.

The discipline — the skill nobody teaches you — is to slow down. Stay in the point. Play the ball in front of you. Not the score, not the set, not the match. Just this ball, right now.

Roger Federer lost 46% of his points. Almost every second point, gone. And he’s arguably the greatest player who ever lived.

He didn’t dwell on the lost point. He focused on what was coming. Dwell later, in the locker room. On the court, the only thing that exists is the next ball.

I’ve lost more games rushing than I ever lost playing steady.

The essence of it — and this took me years to understand — is the cycle. Professional sport is not one speed. It’s two. You spike your heart rate, you explode. Then you drop it. Immediately. Conserve for the next five hours in forty-degree heat. Think Djokovic and Nadal, Australian Open 2012 — five sets, six hours, and neither one rushing.

Slowing down is not losing. Losing is a moment to reflect, regroup, observe. You conserve by learning. You build for the next outburst of energy.

The transition — that’s the skill. Not the explosion. Not the calm. The switch.

I learned this in kitchens before I ever picked up a racket.

When service is falling apart, the chef who starts screaming makes it worse. The one who calls the next ticket calmly brings the whole brigade back.

You don’t fail when it’s super busy — that’s when you have rhythm. You fail on the nights when the rhythm doesn’t come. One dish gets low. One dish gets returned. Nothing catastrophic — just no flow. And you still have to go through.

Being professional is not avoiding those nights. It’s finding a way out when they happen.

My trainer tells me I need more heart on the court. Less analysis, more instinct. He’s probably right. I bring too much of the office — reading patterns, calculating, trying to think three shots ahead when the ball is already moving.

But here’s what I’ve learned from both the court and the kitchen: the answer is never to swing harder. The answer is to move your feet. Adjust. Stay in the point.

The game isn’t over at 2-5. It’s over when you stop playing steady.

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.
