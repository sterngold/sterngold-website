The Lumières Didn't Know They Were Making Art. Neither Does AI.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 3 min read

On December 28, 1895, the Lumière brothers screened ten short films in Paris. Workers leaving a factory. A train arriving at a station. A baby being fed. The newspapers reviewed the device, not the films. Nobody called it art. It was a fairground novelty — vulgar, lowbrow, for people who didn't know better.

AI is in its Lumière phase. It generates text, images, music, code. People look at the output and ask: is this art? Is this going to replace us? The Lumières said cinema had "no future" as entertainment. When Kodak put a camera in everyone's hands in 1888, a critic settled it: "Photography is what the photographer makes it — an art or a trade." Replace "photography" with "AI" and you have today's conversation.

I watched this happen at Danone. We built a marketing mix model — months of pulling data from local countries, cleaning it, organizing it. People who'd been selling for thirty years didn't believe a model could tell them anything. They'd been burned before — expensive agencies, global teams, nothing to show for it. We showed the steering committee a 2-3% improvement. The responses were nasty. They wanted miracles. The percentage became the excuse.

But underneath the number, something else was happening. Once we visualized that data for local marketers, they loved it. In Belgium, a data lead — nobody in that steerCo would have asked her to present — took the granular numbers and made the room see it. The VPs upstairs liked their own voice too much to listen. She didn't. That's when magic happens — when the outspoken ones stop talking and the quiet ones get heard.

Alice Guy-Blaché was a secretary who asked to borrow the Lumière brothers' equipment. Within a year she was directing films. She went on to run her own studio. It took thirty years for cinema to be accepted as art. Thirty years of "it's just moving pictures."

Here's what I wish I'd said in that room: forget the percentage. Your people loved this. They trust you to take the right decision — to continue with this change and make it happen everywhere. This isn't a model problem. It's a people problem. Your people are already ahead of you.

People say coding is dying. I think it's becoming art — the way cinema did after the Lumières, the way photography did after Kodak. The tools get cheaper and faster. The question is still the same: who is brave enough to use them for something they weren't designed for?
