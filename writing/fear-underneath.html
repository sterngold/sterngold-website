I Spent 15 Years Translating Tech to Business. Here's What Nobody Tells You About the Fear in the Room.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

I've sat in hundreds of meetings where someone said "we need to embrace AI" and the room nodded. Everyone agreed. Everyone smiled. And nothing happened.

Not because people are lazy. Not because the strategy was wrong. Because half the room had a knot in their stomach and nobody mentioned it.

I know that knot. I built my career on it.

At Booking.com, we automated the translation and writing of hotel descriptions. The day we presented the solution to the department, I watched the faces of the editors and translators change. Not anger. Something worse. A question nobody had prepared them for: And what about us then? Are we gone?

It was palpable. And of course it didn't happen the way they feared — the transformation brought new roles. But nobody in that room was thinking about new roles. They were thinking about their mortgage. And I was standing on the other side of the table — the side that got to stay. My fear and their fear were not the same fear. I should say that clearly.

That's not a change management problem. That's a human problem. And no PowerPoint deck in the world addresses it.

In the 80s it was personal computers — a beige box on every desk that made corner-office authority feel fragile. Then the internet. Then machine learning, quietly making decisions that used to require senior judgement. Now it's AI, louder and faster than anything before.

The technology changes. The feeling in your stomach doesn't.

When a senior leader pushes back on AI adoption, they're not confused about the business case. They understand the ROI slides. What they've never been given permission to say is: I'm not sure where I fit in this. I built my career on expertise that a machine might approximate. And I don't know what my value is if that expertise becomes a commodity.

I know because I went through my own version of it. Not with AI — with everything.

A few years ago I woke up one morning and couldn't function. I don't mean I felt tired or overwhelmed. I mean I couldn't put my shoe on. Couldn't call anyone. My brain was racing in a downward loop and my body had simply stopped. Three seconds between asleep and broken.

I walked away from a twenty-year relationship. Dismantled my life to find out who I actually was underneath all the roles I was playing. Director. Husband. The person who has it together. I didn't have it together. I was suppressing what I actually needed and calling it professionalism. And when I finally stopped, the whole thing came apart.

And here's the part I never said in any of those rooms where I was the translator, making technology feel manageable for everyone else: I never felt smart enough to be there. Not once. Every meeting, every presentation, every boardroom — I felt like an impostor. The translator who worried he didn't actually speak either language well enough.

Smart people doubt a lot. I know that now. And the best room to be in is the one where you're not the smartest person — but you're heard, you can speak, and you contribute. That took me fifteen years to learn.

AI makes all of this sharper than previous waves. Because AI doesn't just change what you do. It touches who you are in the organization. Your pattern recognition, your years of experience, your gut feeling about what the data means — the things that made you valuable — are suddenly things a machine can get close to. Not replace. But close enough to make you wonder.

The thing about that knot in your stomach: it doesn't go away when you ignore it. It just drives from the back seat. Every resistance to change, every "we need more data before we move forward" — that's fear wearing a business suit.

And fear isn't underneath anything. It's right there. So is shame. We just don't look at it directly.

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.
