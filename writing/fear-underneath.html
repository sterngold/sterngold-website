I Spent 15 Years Translating Tech to Business. Here's What Nobody Tells You About the Discomfort Underneath.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

I've sat in hundreds of meetings where someone said "we need to embrace AI" and the room nodded. Everyone agreed. Everyone smiled. And nothing happened.

Not because people are lazy. Not because the strategy was wrong. Because half the room had a knot in their stomach and nobody mentioned it.

I know that feeling. I built my career on it — being the person who translates between the technical world and the business world. At Booking.com I grew a team from 3 to 50. At Danone I cleaned up 7,000 untracked websites and turned that mess into executive-level policy. My whole job was walking into rooms where technology felt unsettling and making it feel like an opportunity.

But here's what I never said in those rooms: the unease is real. And it's not about the technology. It never was.

In the 80s it was personal computers. People who'd built entire careers on institutional knowledge, Rolodexes, and corner-office authority watched a beige box land on every desk and felt the ground shift. In the 90s it was the internet — suddenly your local expertise competed with the entire world, and "digital" became a word that made seasoned professionals feel like beginners. A few years ago it was machine learning — algorithms quietly making decisions that used to require senior judgement. And now it's AI, louder and faster than anything before.

The technology changes. The feeling in your stomach doesn't.

When a senior leader pushes back on AI adoption, they're not confused about the business case. They understand the ROI slides. What they can't say — what they've never been given permission to say — is: I'm not sure where I fit in this. I built my career on expertise that a machine might approximate. And I don't know what my value is if that expertise becomes a commodity.

That's not a training problem. That's not a change management problem. That's a deeply human thing.

I know because I went through my own version of it. Not with AI — with everything. A few years ago I hit a wall so hard I couldn't function. I walked away from a 20-year relationship. I dismantled my life to find out who I actually was underneath all the roles I was playing. Director. Husband. The person who has it together.

I didn't have it together. I was suppressing what I actually needed and calling it professionalism. And when I finally stopped, the whole thing came apart.

What I learned from that — and what I now see everywhere in corporate life — is that the biggest transformations don't fail because of bad strategy. They fail because people are managing their discomfort instead of naming it. They smile and say "all good" in the meeting. They slow things down in the hallway. They hold on to what they know because the alternative — sitting with not knowing — feels unbearable.

The thing about that knot in your stomach: it doesn't go away when you ignore it. It just drives from the back seat. Every resistance to change, every delayed decision, every "we need more data before we move forward" — that's discomfort wearing a business suit.

And AI makes it sharper than previous waves. Because AI doesn't just change what you do. It touches who you are in the organization. Your pattern recognition, your years of experience, your gut feeling about what the data means — the things that made you valuable — are suddenly things a machine can get close to. Not replace. Get close to. But close enough to make you uneasy.

The way through is not another workshop on prompt engineering. It's not a keynote about the future of work. It's someone sitting across from you and asking: what's actually going on? Not the official version. The real one.

When was the last time someone in your organization admitted they were struggling with a change — and was met with anything other than silence?
