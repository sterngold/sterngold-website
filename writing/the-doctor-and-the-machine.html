The Doctor and the Machine

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

In February 2026, I lay on a dermatologist's table while a machine scanned every mole on my body. Two hundred and seven of them.

The device — a dermatoscope with AI pattern recognition — photographed each one, compared it against millions of training images, and flagged twelve as worth a closer look. It took nine minutes. Tireless, precise, and utterly indifferent to context.

Then the doctor leaned in.

She examined the twelve flagged moles one by one. But she didn't just look at the moles. She looked at me. My skin type. My family history — a grandmother who survived melanoma at sixty-two, a father who spent thirty summers on the Black Sea without sunscreen. The evolution of a mole on my left shoulder she'd been tracking for three years. The fact that I'd recently moved to a country with less UV exposure than the one I grew up in.

The machine saw patterns. The doctor saw causes.

In 2000, a computer scientist named Judea Pearl published a book called Causality. Pearl had spent decades building Bayesian networks — the probabilistic engines behind most AI systems. Then he proved something uncomfortable: those engines have a mathematical ceiling.

Pearl described it as a ladder with three rungs.

Rung 1: Association. Seeing patterns in data. “Patients with this mole shape tend to have melanoma.” This is what the machine does. It's what all machine learning does, no matter how many parameters you add.

Rung 2: Intervention. Asking what happens if you do something. “If we remove this mole, does the risk decrease?” This requires a causal model — a map of what causes what — that no amount of data alone can produce.

Rung 3: Counterfactual. Asking what would have happened. “If this patient had never moved from Sofia to Amsterdam, would this mole have changed?” This requires not just a causal model but individual-level knowledge about a specific person in a specific life.

Each rung is strictly more powerful than the one below. And each rung requires something the lower one cannot provide.

The machine lives on rung 1. My dermatologist lives on rungs 2 and 3.

Here's the part most people get wrong.

The conversation about AI in medicine — and in every profession — is dominated by a comparison of speed. The machine scanned 207 moles in nine minutes. A human doctor would need an hour. The machine is “better.”

That's not a performance comparison. That's a category error.

The machine is faster at pattern recognition. The doctor is doing something the machine mathematically cannot do: reasoning about causes, about this body, about worlds that didn't happen. Pearl proved it. Not as opinion — as theorem.

That's what he called the distinction between seeing and imagining. Machines see. Humans imagine.

I think about this when I coach executives who believe they're being replaced. A marketing director who assumes the AI that writes their copy faster also thinks better. A team lead who measures their worth against an algorithm's throughput. A researcher who watches their analysis get automated and concludes: I'm obsolete.

They're all making the same mistake my dermatoscope would make if it could think. Comparing rung 1 speed to rung 3 depth and declaring itself the winner.

The machine does the math. The human draws the map.

My doctor didn't compete with the dermatoscope. She used it — then did the thing it cannot do. She reasoned about my body, in my life, with my history. That's not a skill that scales with more data. It's a skill that requires a mind.

The question isn't whether the machine is faster than you. It was always faster than you.

The question is: what are you doing on the rungs the machine can't reach?
