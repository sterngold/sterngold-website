I Built My Career on Being the Translator. Then I Ran Out of Languages.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

For 15 years, my job was the same job with different titles: stand between the people who build technology and the people who run the business, and make them understand each other.

At Booking.com, that meant taking a three-person team and growing it to fifty — not because I was the smartest engineer or the sharpest strategist, but because I could sit in a room with both and speak their language. At Danone, it meant walking into a mess of 7,000 untracked websites across dozens of markets, naming the problem nobody wanted to name, and turning it into something the executive committee could act on.

I was the translator. That was my identity. And it worked — for a long time.

Here's what nobody tells you about being the bridge: you spend so much time making other people's worlds make sense that you stop asking whether your own does.

I got good at reading rooms. Knowing who needed reassurance, who needed data, who needed to feel heard before they'd move. That felt like a superpower. And in a way, it was.

But superpowers have a cost. Mine was that I was so focused on translating for everyone else that I forgot to check what I was actually saying for myself. What I wanted. Where I was going — not the team, not the stakeholder, not the organization. Me.

I didn't notice the gap for years. There's always the next crisis, the next reorg, the next big initiative that needs someone who can "make it land." And I kept landing things. I kept being useful. Useful is addictive when you don't want to look at what's underneath.

Then something shifted. We'd been doing ML for years, but when LLMs broke through, suddenly everyone had access to the technology. People could interact with it directly. They got excited. But they forgot that this is still machine language — it still needs learning, it still needs translation. My job didn't disappear. It got harder. Because now I wasn't just translating technology into business outcomes. I was also addressing the huge fear in the corporate room: are we becoming obsolete?

And honestly? I loved it. This was the real translation work. Not just explaining what a model does — but sitting with a roomful of senior people and addressing the fear first, before building anything. That's the part no tool can do.

But it also forced a question I'd been avoiding: if I'm so good at helping others navigate uncertainty, why hadn't I done it for myself? The translator skill isn't the problem. It's a real gift. But I'd been using it as armour. If I'm always the one making things work for others, I never have to figure out what works for me. That's not a career strategy — that's avoidance wearing a nice suit.

I see this pattern everywhere now. The senior leader who's brilliant at aligning teams but hasn't had an honest conversation about their own career in years. The person who's so good at being useful that nobody — including themselves — ever asks: useful for what?

When the skills that defined you start showing up in a tool, you can't just lean on what you know anymore. You have to go deeper — into what you believe, what you care about, what kind of work makes you feel alive rather than just competent. That's a different kind of translation. And most people have never been taught how to do it.

When was the last time you asked yourself — not your boss, not your coach, not your partner — what you actually want? Not what makes sense. What you want.
