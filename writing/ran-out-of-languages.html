I Built My Career on Being the Translator. Then I Ran Out of Languages.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

I arrived at Booking.com as an actual translator. Literal translation — languages, words, meaning across borders. Bulgarian, Hebrew, English, Dutch, German. I'd spent my life collecting languages the way some people collect degrees, each one learned by showing up somewhere I didn't belong and refusing to leave until I could speak.

Then something happened. I stopped translating between languages and started translating between worlds.

A three-person team became fifty — not because I was the smartest engineer or the sharpest strategist, but because I could sit in a room with both and speak their language. At Danone, it meant walking into a mess of 7,000 untracked websites across dozens of markets, naming the problem nobody wanted to name, and turning it into something the executive committee could act on.

I was the translator. That was my identity. And it worked — for a long time.

At Booking, the culture had a phrase for it: show me the data or be quiet. When the CEO said "we should do A or B," the room didn't debate opinions. They asked: do you have facts? Do you have a well-built experiment? Can you test it? That was the language that mattered. And I learned it the way I learned every other language — by doing it, by using it, by getting it wrong until I got it right.

I learned Perl. Then Python. Talking to the machine directly. It was difficult. It was worth it. Because now I wasn't just translating between people — I was translating between people and machines. Data to business facts. Patterns to decisions. Algorithms to something a senior leader could act on without pretending to understand the math.

That felt like a superpower.

Then the vignettes started piling up. A system that could read a human-written review and tell me what it was about — in 2012. Millions of hotel descriptions uploaded without any human interference, and the experiment tool came back positive. It worked. Fraud patterns spotted at a scale no human team could match.

The translator's job didn't disappear. It got stranger.

Because when LLMs broke through, everyone had access to the technology. People could interact with it directly, got excited. But they forgot that this is still machine language — it still needs learning, it still needs someone who can sit between the excitement and the execution. My job got harder. Because now I wasn't just translating technology into business outcomes. I was addressing the huge fear in the corporate room: are we becoming obsolete?

And honestly? I loved it. This was the real translation work. Not explaining what a model does — but sitting with a roomful of senior people and addressing the fear first, before building anything. The part no tool can do.

Here's what nobody tells you about being the bridge: you spend so much time making other people's worlds make sense that you stop asking whether your own does.

I got good at reading rooms. Knowing who needed reassurance, who needed data, who needed to feel heard before they'd move. But superpowers have a cost. Mine was that I was so focused on translating for everyone else that I forgot to check what I was actually saying for myself. What I wanted. Where I was going — not the team, not the stakeholder, not the organization. Me.

I didn't notice the gap for years. There's always the next crisis, the next reorg that needs someone who can "make it land." And I kept landing things. I kept being useful.

But useful is a dangerous word in a world that changes this fast. Useful now. Not useful in a minute. The fundamentals of why something is useful — those don't change. But the surface skills, the ones that feel like identity? Those have a half-life. And if you've built your whole sense of self on being the person who translates, what happens when the machine starts translating too?

You don't run out of languages. You discover there are deeper ones.

I see this pattern everywhere now. The senior leader who's brilliant at aligning teams but hasn't had an honest conversation about their own career in years. The person who's so good at being useful that nobody — including themselves — ever asks: useful for what?

When the skills that defined you start showing up in a tool, you can't just lean on what you know anymore. You have to go deeper — into what you believe, what you care about, what kind of work makes you feel alive rather than just competent. That's a different kind of translation. And most people have never been taught how to do it.

I'm still translating. I think it's a never-ending job. Even when machines do it well — and they do — the human translator works at a higher level. The meaning behind the meaning. The thing the data doesn't say.

When was the last time you asked yourself — not your boss, not your coach, not your partner — what you actually want? Not what makes sense. What you want.

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.
