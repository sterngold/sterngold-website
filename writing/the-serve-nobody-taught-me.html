The Serve Nobody Taught Me

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 3 min read

I play tennis three times a week. Not well — but consistently. And the thing that took me years to learn wasn't the grip, the footwork, or the toss. It was the half-second before the swing.

That's where the match is won or lost. You read the opponent. You pick a direction. And you commit. No hedging. No halfway. You go.

The worst serves I've ever hit were the ones where I changed my mind mid-motion. Tried to split the difference between going wide and going down the T. The ball ends up in the net or floating to exactly where your opponent wanted it. Every time.

I see the same thing in every corporate room I've ever sat in.

A company decides to adopt AI. The business case is solid. The leadership team nods. And then — nothing decisive happens. Instead: another pilot. Another task force. Another "let's align on the framework before we commit resources." Six months later, the competitors moved and you're still workshopping your approach.

That's not strategy. That's a split-second serve where nobody picked a direction.

Here's what tennis taught me: the cost of the wrong shot is almost always lower than the cost of no shot. A serve that goes wide? You adjust. A serve that doesn't leave your hand? That's a free point for the other side.

I've seen this across every world I've worked in — kitchens, agencies, tech companies. The projects that failed weren't the ones that picked the wrong direction. They were the ones that never committed to a direction at all. The ones where everyone agreed in principle and nobody moved in practice. Where the fear of getting it wrong was so strong that the whole room chose the comfortable middle: do nothing, call it caution.

The fear is real. When AI touches your function, your expertise, your team's identity — the instinct is to wait until you're sure. But sure never comes. Not in tennis. Not in transformation. The ball is already in the air.

The difference between leaders who navigate AI well and leaders who get stuck isn't intelligence or information. It's the willingness to commit before they're comfortable.

Next time you're in a meeting where everyone agrees AI is important and nobody decides what to do about it — notice the hesitation. That's a serve dying in mid-air.

What would change if you just picked a direction?

Vlad Sterngold is a digital transformation executive with six careers behind him — from professional kitchens and military service to building data and AI teams at Booking.com and Danone. He writes about what happens to people when machines get better than them.
