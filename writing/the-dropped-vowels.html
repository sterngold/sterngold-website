Bodies & Time

The Dropped Vowels

Vlad Sterngold · March 2026 · 4 min read

In 1865, in Cape Town, a military surgeon died. His name was James Barry — forty-six years in the British Army, one of the first successful cesarean sections on the continent, Inspector General. After he died, the charwoman who prepared the body saw what the Army could not process. James Barry had been assigned female at birth. The Army sealed the records for a hundred years.

I don’t know how Barry felt. Maybe she suffered every day in silence. Maybe she lived a full, precise life in those consonants and wouldn’t have changed a thing. We can’t know. What I know is this: every document, every salute, every record was a context file with the vowels removed. The consonants were enough. VLD. BRR. The system read them, promoted them, trusted them. It never needed the full spelling.

Barry had understood something that I only began to understand in my forties: systems don’t process you. They process your context file. And if the file is wrong — or incomplete — the system either rejects you or rewards a version of you that doesn’t fully exist.

I have written six context files in my life.

In Bulgaria I was the ambitious kid who needed out. In the military I was the soldier who didn’t flinch. In kitchens I was the body that could take the heat — fourteen hours, no complaints, hands like leather. At Booking I was the translator who became a VP. At Danone I was the digital guy who cleaned up the mess.

Each file was true. Each file was compressed.

Hebrew does this. It drops the vowels. אהבה. Four consonants. You’re expected to know the word is ahava — love. The reader reconstructs what’s missing. It works not because the language is broken — but because it trusts the reader to fill in what’s absent. That’s not loss. That’s elegance. The compact form carries everything.

But what happens when the reader fills in vowels that were never yours?

I’ll tell you what happens. They accumulate.

The queerness I didn’t perform in the boardroom — sitting in a meeting, twelve people around a table, and I’m watching myself from the outside: how I hold my hands, how I lower my voice half a register, how I laugh at the joke I’d never make. That’s not strategy. That’s compression so deep you forget you’re doing it.

The tenderness I didn’t show in kitchens. The version of me that was too much for every system I entered — too fluid, too hungry, too queer.

You compress long enough and you think the vowels are gone. You think VLD is who you are. Efficient. Legible. Fast to load.

Then one morning you can’t get out of bed and you don’t know why. Or you can. You get out of bed and you go to the gym and you perform the consonants perfectly and nobody notices that the word has stopped meaning anything. The compression holds until it doesn’t. And when it breaks, it doesn’t break quietly.

But here’s what I know now that I didn’t know at thirty, at twenty-five, at nineteen standing in a kitchen in Amsterdam with my jaw clenched:

The dropped vowels don’t disappear. They wait.

Barry’s vowels waited forty-six years. Mine waited thirty.

Now I wear mascara to sex-positive parties and I feel more like myself than I ever did in a suit presenting quarterly results. I practice polyamory and it makes more sense to me than anything a marriage counsellor ever said. I dress up. I show up. I let the full word load.

This isn’t rebellion. Rebellion is against something. This is decompression. This is the vowels coming home.

Maybe compression is a technology we need. Barry needed it. I needed it. Every queer person who ever read a room and adjusted — we are experts in compression. We’ve been doing it since before anyone gave it a name. There is an elegance in it — the way a life can be carried in so few letters, the way the right reader sees the whole word.

But I want to say this clearly, to whoever is compressing right now, in whatever system, for whatever reason:

Know your vowels. Name them, even if only to yourself. Write them down somewhere no system will read. Because the thing about compression is that it works — until the day you forget that the short version isn’t the whole word.

And on that day, you will need the full spelling. Every letter. Every breath that didn’t fit.

אהבה.
Love.
The word that trusts you to know what it means — without ever spelling it out.

Sources

  • James Barry (surgeon) (c. 1789–1865), British Army surgeon, Inspector General. Served forty-six years; performed one of the first successful cesarean sections in Africa. Assigned female at birth — discovered post-mortem. See Rachel Holmes, The Secret Life of Dr James Barry (Tempus, 2002).
  • Hebrew is an abjad — a writing system where consonants are written and vowels are largely omitted, reconstructed by the reader from context. See Geoffrey Khan, The Semitic Languages (Routledge, 2024).
