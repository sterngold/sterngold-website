Bodies & Time

The Dropped Vowels

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

In 1865, in Cape Town, a military surgeon died. His name was James Barry. He had served forty-six years in the British Army. Performed one of the first successful cesarean sections on the continent. Rose to Inspector General — the highest medical rank the Empire could offer. Forty-six years of bodies opened and sutured, of rank earned, of men saluted.

After he died, the charwoman who prepared the body saw what the Army could not process.

James Barry had been assigned female at birth.

The Army sealed the records for a hundred years.

I think about that charwoman. Not Barry — the charwoman. The moment she looked at a body and realised that every document, every salute, every record of service was a context file with the vowels removed. The consonants were enough. VLD. BRR. The system read them, promoted them, trusted them. It never needed the full spelling.

Barry had understood something that I only began to understand in my forties: systems don't process you. They process your context file. And if the file is wrong — or incomplete — the system either rejects you or rewards a version of you that doesn't fully exist.

I have written six context files in my life.

In Bulgaria I was the ambitious kid who needed out. In the military I was the soldier who didn't flinch. In kitchens I was the body that could take the heat — fourteen hours, no complaints, hands like leather. At Booking I was the translator who became a VP. At Danone I was the digital guy who cleaned up the mess.

Each file was true. Each file was compressed.

Hebrew does this. It drops the vowels. אהבה. Four consonants. You're expected to know the word is ahava — love. The reader reconstructs what's missing. It works because there's a shared grammar, a cultural operating system that fills in the gaps.

But what happens to the vowels?

I'll tell you what happens. They accumulate.

The softness I didn't show in the kitchen. The queerness I didn't perform in the boardroom. The desire I didn't name in my marriage. The mascara I didn't wear. The parties I didn't attend. The lovers I didn't take. The version of me that was too much for every system I entered — too fluid, too hungry, too queer, too tender.

You compress long enough and you think the vowels are gone. You think VLD is who you are. Efficient. Legible. Fast to load.

Then one morning you can't get out of bed and you don't know why. Or you can. You get out of bed and you go to the gym and you perform the consonants perfectly and nobody notices that the word has stopped meaning anything. That's what happens. The compression holds until it doesn't. And when it breaks, it doesn't break quietly.

I build context files now. Literal ones. On my computer, there are markdown files that tell an AI who I am — my values, my goals, my daily rhythms, my blind spots. When I start a session, the machine reads them. It knows me. Not because it remembers — it has no memory. Because the context is loaded fresh, every time, in milliseconds.

I've been thinking about this architecture.

The AI doesn't know me. It processes a compressed version of me. And the compression is deliberate. I chose what goes in. I chose what stays out. The machine reconstructs the rest — just like Hebrew, just like Barry's Army, just like every system I've ever handed a version of myself to.

But here's what I know now that I didn't know at thirty, at twenty-five, at nineteen standing in a kitchen in Amsterdam with my jaw clenched:

The dropped vowels don't disappear. They wait.

Barry's vowels waited forty-six years. Mine waited thirty.

Now I wear mascara to sex-positive parties and I feel more like myself than I ever did in a suit presenting quarterly results. I practice polyamory and it makes more sense to me than anything a marriage counsellor ever said. I dress up. I show up. I let the full word load.

This isn't rebellion. Rebellion is against something. This is decompression. This is the vowels coming home.

I think the future is context files. Not just for AI — for everything. You'll walk into a job interview and hand them your .ctx — a compressed version of you, optimised for their system. They'll process it. They'll see the consonants. They'll reconstruct what they expect to find between them.

And maybe that's fine. Maybe compression is a technology we need. Barry needed it. I needed it. Every queer person who ever read a room and adjusted — we are experts in context engineering. We've been doing it since before the machines.

But I want to say this clearly, to whoever is compressing right now, in whatever system, for whatever reason:

Know your vowels. Name them, even if only to yourself. Write them down somewhere no system will read. Because the thing about compression is that it works — until the day you forget that the short version isn't the whole word.

And on that day, you will need the full spelling. Every letter. Every breath that didn't fit.

אהבה.
Love.
The word that has never once been efficient.

Vlad Sterngold writes about bodies, identity, and the versions of ourselves that systems can't process. Six careers across professional kitchens, the military, advertising, a startup, and digital leadership at Booking.com and Danone.
