Your AI Transformation Program Is 113 Years Old

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 3 min read

In 1911, Frederick Winslow Taylor published The Principles of Scientific Management. He sent engineers with stopwatches to factory floors, timed every human movement, and eliminated inefficiency. Skilled machinists who had spent years developing their craft were redesigned — not their tools, but the people — until each worker fit optimally into a system Taylor could measure and improve.

His summary: "In the past the man has been first; in the future the system must be first."

It worked. Productivity went up. Pride in craft went somewhere else.

The pattern didn't stop with factories. Ford's assembly line synchronized workers to the conveyor belt's rhythm, not their own. Office management in the 1950s applied the same logic to knowledge work — typing pools, standardized procedures, efficiency consultants measuring how quickly clerks processed paper. Then email arrived, which meant the system could reach you at any hour. Then dashboards, OKRs, and performance metrics calibrated to outputs a machine could count.

Each wave: new technology, same instinct. Adjust the human. Measure the result. Repeat.

Günther Anders saw this pattern and named it in 1956. He called it "the welcome of our own reification" — humans voluntarily reshaping their values, their pace, their sense of what counts as contribution, to match the machine's standards. Not under duress. Because the machine is so evidently better at certain things that being human starts to feel like the problem that needs solving.

I've spent fifteen years in digital leadership — at Booking.com, at Danone — watching exactly this play out with every technology cycle. With AI, it's moving faster. But the shape is identical.

A team lead measures their people's output against ChatGPT's response time and calls this "setting expectations." A senior analyst quietly adds disclaimers to her work: "this used to take me a week." A marketing director redesigns his entire workflow around daily output volume because that's what efficiency looks like now. Nobody instructed them to hold themselves to machine standards. The machine set a new baseline, and human pace started feeling like human failure.

That's not AI transformation. That's Taylor with a ChatGPT subscription.

Most AI programs I've seen run on the same underlying question: how do we reshape the human to get more from the machine? New tools. New workshops. New dashboards. Old logic.

The question I keep returning to — and the one almost nobody is asking — is: what do we want the machine to make possible for the human?

Not what humans can do faster alongside AI. What can they do deeper? What gets freed up? What gets given back to them?

That's a genuinely different project. Very few organizations are running it.

When your AI strategy lands, what will people in your organization be able to do that they couldn't before — not faster, but differently?

If you don't have a clear answer, Taylor is still running the meeting.

Sources

  • Frederick Winslow Taylor, The Principles of Scientific Management (Harper & Brothers, 1911). The quote "In the past the man has been first; in the future the system must be first" is from the introduction.
  • Gunther Anders, Die Antiquiertheit des Menschen, Vol. 1 (C.H. Beck, 1956). The concept of humans voluntarily reshaping themselves to match the machine — what Anders called welcoming our own reification — appears in Part I on Promethean shame.
  • Henry Ford's assembly line at Highland Park (1913) and its impact on worker autonomy is documented in David A. Hounshell, From the American System to Mass Production, 1800–1932 (Johns Hopkins University Press, 1984).
