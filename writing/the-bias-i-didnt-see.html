I Write About Dehumanization. I Was Doing It in My Own Sentences.

Vlad Sterngold · February 2026 · 4 min read

In 1896, a secretary at the Gaumont film company asked her boss if she could borrow a camera. Within a year, Alice Guy-Blaché was directing narrative films — among the first ever made. She went on to run her own studio, direct over a thousand films, and pioneer techniques that shaped cinema for decades. Then she was systematically erased from the record. For most of the twentieth century, her work was attributed to male colleagues or simply forgotten.

I wrote a piece about overlooked innovators a few weeks ago. About how the people who transform a technology are never the ones the industry expects. I used Georges Méliès as my example. I left Guy-Blaché out entirely.

A piece about who gets overlooked — and I overlooked her.

That's when I decided to audit everything I'd written.

Ten pieces. Every historical authority figure: male. Günther Anders, Frederick Taylor, the Lumière brothers, Méliès. In one piece I'd written that Anders had “married Hannah Arendt” — one of the twentieth century's most important political philosophers, and in my sentence she was a wife. A biographical footnote to a man's story.

Every woman who appeared in a corporate vignette was reactive. The analyst who watches ChatGPT do her work in ten seconds — she internalizes it: “I should be faster.” The team lead who measures her people against AI output — she adjusts without being asked. The men in my vignettes built things, commanded things, went quiet with dignity. The women absorbed shame. The men performed stoicism.

I hadn't decided this. That's the point. Nobody sits down and thinks: I'll give the women the self-blame and the men the silence. It's the water you swim in. You reproduce the pattern because the pattern is what “normal” looks like.

Zero mentions of race across ten pieces. Zero mentions of disability, sexuality, or religion. Every unnamed character defaulted to what readers picture when you don't specify: white, Western, able-bodied, straight. I wasn't choosing that. I was not-choosing it, which is the same thing.

I write about what happens to people when the machines get better than them. But my “people” were one kind of person. The thesis was universal. The cast was not.

This is not a confession piece. I'm not performing guilt for your approval. I'm naming a pattern because naming patterns is what I do — and this time the pattern was mine.

Here's what I think it means for anyone doing this kind of work:

The default is invisible until you look. When you write “the senior analyst” without specifying gender, your reader fills it in. When every authority figure in your writing is a European man, you're not being neutral — you're being specific without realizing it. Defaults are choices. The most powerful ones are the ones you never notice making.

Representation isn't decoration. Adding diverse examples to satisfy a checklist weakens the work. But asking “whose experience am I centering, and whose am I erasing?” makes the argument stronger. A piece about dehumanization that only portrays one kind of human undermines itself. I was undermining my own thesis and I couldn't see it because the bias was in my blind spot, not in my intent.

Intent doesn't matter as much as you think. I care about this. I think about power, about who gets seen and who doesn't. And I still reproduced the pattern. Not because I'm careless. Because defaults are structural, not personal. You can't think your way out of a bias you're not measuring.

I went back and revised the pieces. The analyst became “them” — because gender was irrelevant to the point. The team lead became “he” — a man measuring his people against the machine, adjusting without being asked. The marketing director became “she” — a woman in authority, going quiet not from self-blame but from something harder to name. Arendt became “fellow philosopher.” Guy-Blaché got her paragraph back.

Small changes. Not one argument weakened. Several got sharper — because the vignettes stopped confirming what the reader already assumed and started making them look twice.

What would you find if you audited the last ten things you published — not for what you said, but for who you made visible and who you left out?
